# Strategy & Adoption

Strategy precedes tools. Culture, not technology, determines success (tech is ~20%).

## Two KM strategies

- **Codification** — capture knowledge in docs/wikis for reuse without the original expert. Best for **explicit** knowledge (processes, procedures, reference). Risk: unused content that lacks context or is hard to find.
- **Personalization** — connect people to people via mentoring, communities, expert directories. Best for **tacit** knowledge (judgment, experience). Risk: knowledge leaves when people leave.
- Most orgs need both. The wiki mainly serves codification but should also link users to subject-matter experts.

## Set goals before building

1. **What problem are we solving?** Slow onboarding, repeat questions consuming experts, knowledge loss when people leave, siloed/duplicated work.
2. **Who creates vs. consumes** the knowledge, and how do they seek it?
3. **What does success look like?** Measurable outcomes (time-to-answer, fewer repeat questions, faster onboarding) plus leading indicators (views, search success, contribution rate).
4. **What are the constraints?** Maintenance capacity, tooling, governance appetite.

**Rule:** Adoption follows value. Start with the highest-pain gap, pilot with a willing team, prove value fast, then expand. Don't build a comprehensive system before users are on board.

## Explicit vs. tacit knowledge

| Type | Characteristics | Capture via |
|---|---|---|
| **Explicit** | Documented, transferable, structured | Wikis, documents, databases |
| **Tacit** | Experiential, judgment-based, hard to articulate | Stories, mentoring, peer learning, communities |

A wiki is primarily for explicit knowledge; don't expect it to replace conversation.

## Knowledge lifecycle

Create → Capture → **Organize** → Share → **Reuse** → **Review** → Retire. Most orgs under-invest in Organize, Reuse, and Review.

## KM maturity levels

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 — Ad Hoc | No structure; knowledge in email, notes, Slack |
| 2 — Reactive | Some shared storage; minimal organization |
| 3 — Defined | Structured wiki with taxonomy; governance beginning |
| 4 — Managed | Consistent templates, metadata, ownership; regular reviews |
| 5 — Optimizing | Analytics-driven; integrated with workflows; sharing culture |

Build incrementally; don't jump from Level 1 to 5.

## Why people don't share

| Barrier | Root cause |
|---|---|
| "It's not my job" | KM not recognized as normal work |
| "I don't have time" | Contribution feels like extra work |
| "I'll lose my value" | Hoarding as job security |
| "It's already out there" | No gap visibility |
| "No one uses it anyway" | No feedback loop showing impact |
| "I don't know where to put it" | Confusing structure |

## What drives sharing

Psychological safety · reciprocity (seeing others share) · social recognition/attribution · intrinsic motivation (don't over-rely on extrinsic rewards) · low friction · clear ownership.

## Leader behaviors (the biggest cultural lever)

**Do:** model sharing; recognize contributors publicly; reference the wiki in meetings as the source of truth; remove barriers; never penalize imperfect contributions.
**Kills culture:** asking for docs but never using them; making uncertainty unsafe; treating the wiki as an audit trail.

## Adoption: Seed → Grow → Embed → Sustain

1. **Seed** — build high-quality initial content with a small team; get one influential person using it.
2. **Grow** — recruit champions per team; make contribution the easiest path (templates, clear "where does this go?"); celebrate early contributors.
3. **Embed** — make wiki updates part of project/sprint completion and on/off-boarding; make it the authoritative source.
4. **Sustain** — regular analytics reviews, ongoing recognition, governance cadence to prevent decay.

## Incentives: what works

| Approach | Effectiveness |
|---|---|
| Individual financial rewards | Low (drives low-quality volume) |
| Team recognition / shout-outs | Medium-High |
| Attribution on pages | High |
| Making the wiki central to work (not optional) | High |
| Seeing your contributions used | High |
| Leaderboards / gamification | Variable (can backfire) |

## Managing resistance

- **"We already have email/Slack/SharePoint"** → position the wiki as the *destination*, not a competitor to conversation; make it better than alternatives, not just mandated.
- **"I don't have time to document"** → reduce friction (templates, capture during work); make it part of "definition of done"; show it saves repeat-answer time.
- **"The wiki is always out of date"** → this is a *governance* problem (see [07-governance.md](07-governance.md)): ownership, review cycles, lifecycle states.

**Virtuous cycle:** good culture → good content → usage → demonstrated value → reinforced culture. Starting with culture is most durable.
